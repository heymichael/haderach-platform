## Context

The `haderach-home` repo needs a public-facing homepage and blog. The homepage should communicate what Haderach is and why it exists. The blog should be a lightweight, easy-to-maintain publishing surface for thoughts on how we're building the platform and the principles behind it.

## What needs to happen

- Design and build a homepage with clear positioning
- Add a blog section (static generation or lightweight CMS)
- Publish initial posts

## Blog ideas

- **The importance of fast analytics performance** — Why sub-second query response times change how people interact with data. How pre-processed Firestore documents and a thin MCP layer beat traditional BI for operational questions.
- **Pushing development out to the practitioners** — Why the people closest to the work should be able to shape their own tools. How agentic interfaces and low-code patterns let business users drive capability without waiting on engineering.
- **Loops, not flowcharts** — Why real work doesn't follow linear workflows. How feedback loops, iterative conversations, and agent-driven interactions model actual business processes better than rigid step-by-step automation.
- **Fine-grained permissioning for the agentic era** — Why usable, granular permissions are critical for highly automated business processes. Agents need scoped access to data and actions just like human users do — and internal users need clear, manageable controls over what agents can see and do on their behalf.
- **The importance of process telemetry** — When more and more happens automatically, you lose the implicit visibility that manual steps used to provide. Telemetry and monitoring become critical — not just for debugging, but for trust, auditability, and knowing that automated processes are doing what you expect at the pace you expect.
- **Less politics, more signal** — Modern process should make strategy legible. Thoughts and meanderings are shared in raw form, not polished into decks after the fact. Conflicts and positional plays get surfaced — not to accuse, but as a natural part of how decisions are made in the open. People with genuinely successful strategies get recognized on their merits, not gated behind whether a manager chose to amplify them.
